Photinus marginellus (Little Gray Firefly)

Season: mid-June to early August
Time: 9:30 PM - 10:00 PM

Photinus marginellus, is a common flashing firefly species occurring across much of the eastern United States and Canada. This species occurs in small isolated colonies. In the Northeast, it is reportedly common, particularly at multiple sites in Massachusetts and Ontario.

Photinus marginellus are small nocturnal fireflies that emit single flashes. During courtship, males emit quick singlt flashes lasting less than a third of a second, repeated every four or five seconds. Earlier in the evening males flash while flying just over a meter off the ground, but later in the night, towards the end of their 45 minute activity period, flashing takes place several meters off the ground. Females on the ground or perched on low vegetation respond to male signals with a single flash given at a short time delay. They are found in forests and forest margins in valleys and mountainous regions. It has also been reported from lawns, and along roadsides and streams in shrubs and hedges.




Photuris versicolor (Predatory firefly)

Season: June and July
Time: 9:30 PM - 11:00 PM

Photuris versicolor, a species of predatory fireflies, is a common species of firefly found in a diversity of habitats from grasslands and forests to lawns and fields. It is widespread across the northeastern United States. Females of this species are predatory and are capable of dispersing to hunt and find appropriate egg-laying habitats.

The Photuris species are predatory fireflies, often coined “femme fatale.” prey on male fireflies of other species. The females are able to entrap the males by faking the flash signal characteristics of female fireflies of the other species. By feeding on males of other firefly species, Photuris females gain more than nutrients, they also acquire defnesive toxins that Photuris fireflies are unable to produce on their own. These toxins protect fireflies from predatory spiders.

While Photuris females have variable flash patterns because they imitate the flash patterns of other fireflies to trap them, Photuris males produce a very fast triple flash, followed by a pause of around 5 seconds and repeated. This species is most common in June and July. It is a widespread and adaptable species that prefers many different types of habitats. It is a large firefly 0.5 inches long.
